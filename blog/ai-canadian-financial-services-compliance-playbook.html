AI for Canadian Financial Services: The Compliance-First Playbook

In 2026, the FIs that win aren't running the smartest models. They're running the most auditable ones.

By Srivatsa Kasagar · Feb 14, 2026 · 14 min read
Share: LinkedIn X

The thesis

AI in Canadian financial services is now a compliance-first discipline.

That probably sounds boring compared to the "AI will transform everything" pitch you've been hearing since 2023. I spent 25 years in financial services, and only recently started building AI tools for this market. But the pattern I keep seeing is familiar: the institutions that move fastest on AI adoption are the ones that get audited first. Provable governance is the actual competitive advantage.

Here's why. OSFI published Guideline E-23 in September 2025, effective May 2027. It's the first Canadian regulation that explicitly treats AI and machine learning models as regulated artifacts, requiring lifecycle governance, explainability assessments, and documented rationale for every model a federally regulated financial institution deploys. At the same time, FINTRAC hosted FIFAI II in October 2025, bringing together 60+ participants from across Canada's financial ecosystem to figure out how AI fits into anti-money laundering operations. And agentic AI — systems that don't just generate text but take autonomous actions — is already being piloted in KYC reviews and SAR preparation across Canadian banks.

A lot happened in 2025, and most institutions are still processing it.

What changed: from generative hype to agentic utility

The first wave of AI in finance was generative. Summarize this document. Draft this email. Analyze this contract. The model produces text, a human reads it, end of story. Useful, but fundamentally a productivity tool.

The second wave, which is happening now, is agentic. AI systems that execute multi-step workflows: pull data from three systems, cross-reference it, flag anomalies, draft a report, and route it for human approval. The model is doing work, not producing a document for someone else to act on.

This changes the compliance math entirely. When AI generates a draft memo, the worst case is a bad memo. When an agentic system autonomously files a Suspicious Activity Report, the worst case is a false filing to FINTRAC that triggers an investigation, or a missed filing that constitutes a regulatory breach. Those are different categories of risk, and they demand different governance.

Compliance-first isn't a slogan here. It's a precondition for deploying agentic AI in Canadian financial services without creating more risk than you're trying to manage.

OSFI E-23: what it actually requires

I've read through the full guideline and the Torys, Blakes, and BLG analyses. Here's what matters for AI teams at Canadian FIs.

E-23 applies to all models at FRFIs, regardless of whether they're built internally or sourced from a vendor. The definition of "model" is deliberately broad — any methodology that processes input data to generate results. AI and ML are explicitly included.

Four requirements stand out:

Lifecycle governance

Every model needs documented rationale covering its purpose, the specific business use case, and a risk assessment of its intended usage. This applies at design, development, deployment, monitoring, and retirement. You can't just ship a model and forget about it.

Explainability assessment

FRFIs must evaluate the level of explainability appropriate for each model's use case. For AI/ML models specifically, OSFI expects institutions to consider "the level of transparency and explainability required, and the need for alternative controls, especially for black box approaches or autonomous models." That last phrase is OSFI's way of acknowledging that agentic AI is coming and it needs guardrails before it arrives.

Data quality standards

Training data must be accurate, relevant, representative, compliant, traceable, and timely. If you're fine-tuning a model on Canadian financial data, you need to document where that data came from, whether it's representative, and whether it introduces bias.

Third-party model governance

If your AI comes from a vendor (which for most FIs, it does), E-23 layers onto OSFI B-10 (third-party risk management). You're responsible for the model's governance even if you didn't build it. Your vendor needs to provide explainability documentation, data lineage, and ongoing monitoring capabilities — or you need to build those controls yourself.

Timeline

The compliance deadline is May 2027. That sounds far away until you consider that building an enterprise-wide model risk management framework from scratch takes 12–18 months. The work needs to start now.

The agentic accountability stack

If your institution is piloting agentic AI — or planning to — you need an accountability framework that goes beyond what generative AI required. I've been thinking about this as a four-layer stack:

Layer What it does Why it matters
Action log Records every action the agent takes — not just the final output, but every intermediate step: data accessed, APIs called, decisions made Your evidence trail when FINTRAC or OSFI asks "why did your system do that?"
Decision trace Connects each decision to the specific data that informed it. "Flagged because amount exceeded $10K and beneficiary matched PEP list" — that level of specificity E-23's explainability requirement for autonomous models. "The AI determined it was risky" won't survive an audit
Human approval gate High-consequence actions require human sign-off before execution. Agent drafts a SAR, compliance officer files it. Configurable by action type and risk level Keeps humans accountable for regulated decisions. The agent assists; it doesn't decide
Immutable audit record Action logs, decision traces, and approval records go into an append-only store that can't be modified after the fact Regulatory evidence. If it's mutable, it's worthless in an enforcement proceeding

This stack isn't theoretical. It's what E-23's explainability and documentation requirements demand for autonomous models, and it's the architecture I'm building into LocalMind's review and analysis pipeline.

Agentic AML: the use case that's already here

Anti-money laundering is where agentic AI is moving fastest in Canadian financial services. AML investigations are labor-intensive, time-sensitive, and follow structured enough workflows that an agent can handle most of the steps. A typical SAR investigation involves pulling transaction records, cross-referencing against watchlists, analyzing patterns, drafting a narrative, and submitting to FINTRAC. Each step is well-defined.

SymphonyAI and Hawk have both launched agentic AML products in the past year. Chartis reports that 85% of institutions expect to increase agentic AI investment in AML over the next two to three years. Early numbers look good: 50% reduction in investigation time per case, roughly two hours of analyst labor saved per investigation.

But here's what I keep coming back to. An agentic system that pre-fills a SAR narrative is doing regulated work. If that narrative contains errors, omissions, or false positives that lead to a bad filing, the institution is liable. Not the AI vendor. Not the model. The institution.

That's why the accountability stack matters. The agent does the legwork, but the compliance officer sees the decision trace, reviews the evidence, and makes the call. The audit record proves this happened. FINTRAC's enforcement history in 2025 — 20 AMPs, among the highest in a single year — shows they're not going easy on institutions that get AML compliance wrong, regardless of what tools they use.

If you're building AML tooling for the Canadian market, the accountability stack is the product. The AI part is almost secondary.

The sovereignty question

I've written about data sovereignty in detail in my PIPEDA compliance guide, and about CLOUD Act exposure in my comparison of ChatGPT vs self-hosted AI for law firms. I won't repeat those arguments here. The short version: US-incorporated infrastructure operators are compellable under the CLOUD Act regardless of where data sits.

For financial services specifically, there's an additional wrinkle. OSFI B-13 already requires FRFIs to assess third-party technology risk. E-23 extends this to AI models and their data. If your agentic AI system processes customer transaction data through a US-operated model, you need to document the sovereignty exposure in your model risk assessment. "We use AWS Canada region" is not sufficient documentation.

The Canadian sovereign cloud market has matured in the last year. ThinkOn/Hypertec launched their government-grade sovereign cloud in October 2025. TELUS/OpenText went live in July 2025. Bell/SAP followed in February 2026. For institutions that need Tier 1 sovereignty (Canadian-incorporated operators only), the options exist now. They didn't a year ago.

I built LocalMind on Cloudflare's edge stack — Workers AI for embeddings, Vectorize for search, R2 for storage — with Canadian routing. It's Tier 2 sovereignty (US-incorporated but processing pinned to Canada), and I'm transparent about that tradeoff. For most use cases outside national security and government litigation, Tier 2 with proper documentation satisfies OSFI's requirements. But you have to make the assessment and document it. That's the part most institutions skip.

What to do now

If you're at a Canadian FI or building tools for one, here's where I'd focus between now and May 2027:

Map your AI inventory. You probably have more models in production than you think. Spreadsheets with regression formulas count under E-23's broad definition. Vendor-provided AI in your fraud detection, credit scoring, and customer service tools count. Start with a complete inventory.

Assess each model's risk tier. Not every model needs the same level of governance. A model that recommends marketing offers needs less oversight than one that approves credit applications or flags suspicious transactions. E-23 expects risk-proportionate governance.

Build the accountability stack for agentic pilots. If you're running agentic AI in any capacity, implement the four layers (action log, decision trace, human approval gate, immutable audit record) now. Don't wait for E-23's effective date. The framework you build during pilots becomes your production architecture.

Document sovereignty decisions. For every AI model and data pipeline, document where processing happens, who operates the infrastructure, and what your CLOUD Act exposure is. This documentation will be required under E-23 and is already expected under B-13.

Start with AML. Clearest ROI, most structured workflow, and highest regulatory urgency given FINTRAC's enforcement posture. It's also where getting accountability wrong has the most immediate consequences, so you'll learn fast what your governance framework can and can't handle.
