NordID and the Canadian business identity gap

Mar 12, 2026 · Working note — will evolve

Every KYC platform selling into Canada hits the same wall. Authoritative Canadian business data is scattered across ISED (federal corporations), 13 provincial registries, the CRA (GST/HST status), the ISC registry for individuals with significant control, sanctions screening providers, and bank account aggregators. Nothing stitches it together. Compliance teams do it manually in spreadsheets. The global KYC vendors — Persona, ComplyCube, Sumsub, Onfido — return a generic pass/fail with no FINTRAC rule citations, no Canadian data residency by architecture, and no unified ultimate-beneficial-ownership tree. FINTRAC’s October 2025 UBO discrepancy reporting obligation made this worse: reporting entities are now legally required to flag where the UBO a business self-declared diverges from what the ISC registry says, and nobody has a clean data feed to do it with.

The research observation driving this build: KYC platforms should not be the ones building data plumbing. Persona does not want to maintain 13 provincial registry integrations. Canadian credit unions and PSPs do not want to decide which registry record is authoritative when the federal CBCA record says one address and the Ontario extra-provincial record says another. This is data-and-intelligence-layer work — the layer underneath KYC, not KYC itself. The ground is also shifting in ways that make an infrastructure play timely: FINTRAC is tightening reporting rules, the ISC registry is now publicly queryable as of 2024, the Retail Payment Activities Act brought new regulated entities into scope, and every Canadian fintech board is asking the same question about data residency.

What I’m building in the lab: NordID — api.nordid.ca — a Canadian business identity API platform that unifies five families: Registry (does this business legally exist?), Tax (is it actively trading with CRA?), Ownership (who controls it, with FINTRAC-aligned UBO discrepancy scoring), Screen (sanctions and PEP lists), and Comply (a FINTRAC-rule-cited CDD decision, not a generic score). It runs entirely on Cloudflare’s Canadian edge — Workers, KV, R2 with jurisdiction: ca, D1 for append-only audit — so every response can truthfully return data_residency: "CANADA_CONFIRMED". Deliberately positioned as infrastructure, not a compliance authority: NordID provides compliance intelligence; the FINTRAC-regulated reporting entity still makes the final determination. Phase 1 (Registry + Tax) ships first, with universal ID resolution across BN9, NEQ, BIN, and provincial corp numbers. A later phase (Verified Corporate Wallet) explores turning verified business identity into a reusable credential. The open research question: does a data-layer API with cited regulatory rules underneath every response shift how Canadian KYC feels — or do buyers still prefer a single vendor that owns the full stack? If you’re running KYB in Canada and this sounds familiar, I’d like to compare notes.
